DataHub Editorial Style Guide
Last updated: Nov 19, 2025

Tone Guidelines
The DataHub Voice in Content
The DataHub voice sounds like an experienced architect explaining why foundations matter. We don't oversell or hype. We use precise technical language when it matters and plain language everywhere else. We are direct about trade-offs and implementation realities. 
Our tone is confident but not arrogant, substantive without being erudite. We lead with practitioner insights and concrete outcomes over marketing claims. We choose strategic clarity over buzzwords, and we prove value through specific examples rather than abstract promises.
Writing Principles
The #1 Marketing Writing Standard
Before you start writing, identify the value to your intended audience
What is their problem that you will solve?
Start with that challenge you will solve. Don’t start with what you want them to do, otherwise it feels too transactional
Quick-Hit Guidelines
Say what you mean and be accurate. Don’t exaggerate, or lie
All call-to-actions (CTAs) and headlines must reflect the ultimate value proposition. Don’t write click-bait. Don’t overpromise and underdeliver
Lead content with your main and most important point. Don’t bury the lede
Keep to one tense
Be concise. Writing a clear, concise, and specific statement is harder than writing a long, nebulous, jargony sentence
Impactful Writing Rules
These rules are quick and easy ways to make any sentence, paragraph, or article clear and powerful.
Never use a long word where a short one will do
If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out
Never use the passive where you can use the active
“If you can append ‘by zombies’ to the end of a sentence, you’ve written a sentence in the passive voice.” (From Dreyer’s English)
The active is usually more direct, persuasive, and concise than the passive
Marketing is about inspiring action, so we should write for action. Use strong action verbs where possible
Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday equivalent, unless necessary to communicate the topic at hand
Style Instructions
Get right into what you want to say, you don’t need to “clear your throat”—add fluff—in the beginning. When in doubt, ask yourself if the first sentence in your content is really necessary
Address directly with “you” where it makes sense. This builds a personal connection and clearly articulates that you are speaking to their  benefit
Rule of three: When words or phrases are grouped together in threes, they add rhythm, life, and color to the sentence. (See what I did there?)
Please note: Balance being concise with this more rhythmic writing. Ultimately, use your best judgment, but one good tip is to not have too many “rule of three” sentences in a row. And only use it when it really makes sense. Don’t include more words if they are not all additive
Grammar
Follow the rules laid out in this section to ensure we have consistent grammar across all our content.
Please note: sometimes we break AP rules (specifically punctuation) because we opt for better clarity that other styles provide. But when in doubt on grammar/style, if not included in this guide, then default to AP rules.
Abbreviations and acronyms
Write out the entire name the first time, with an abbreviation in parentheses if you plan to use it again
For example: “Model Context Protocol (MCP)” or “Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)”
Too many capital letters can make it hard for a reader to remember or follow, so keep abbreviations and acronyms to a minimum
Don’t spell out well known acronyms like “CEO” or “AI”
Don’t use periods between letters in capital abbreviations
For example: US, not U.S.
Use periods in lower case abbreviations. For example: “e.g.”
Exception: Don’t use for AM/PM
We avoid using ampersands (&) – write out “and” every time, unless you are referring to a product or title that already uses it, or are limited for space (e.g. X post, meta description)
Capitalization
We do not capitalize the disciplines or departments: engineering, product, sales
Headlines: Use sentence case. Not Title Case
Exception for H1s/Titles
Section headers: Use sentence case. Not Title Case 
In presentations, for headlines and breaker slides: Use sentence case. Not Title Case
Dates
Use the following written format when expressing dates and abbreviate months longer than five letters: March 3, 2026 // Dec. 2, 2025
Correct format for all months: Jan, Feb, March, April, May, June, July, Aug, Sept, Oct, Nov, Dec
Don’t abbreviate the month if you are using it in a sentence. For example “in September we are rolling out a new feature.”
Don’t use numeric dates (3/3/23). Some countries write numerically with day first and others put the month first. To avoid any confusion, name the month (shown above)
Formatting
Use italics for: works of art and culture—books, films, movies, TV series, albums, etc.
For example: Fundamentals of Data Engineering by Joe Reis
Don’t use italics for: news outlets or websites.
For example: Forrester, Gartner, Forbes
Bolding: Use it sparingly. Don’t overdo it
Reserve bolding for where you really want to draw the reader’s attention or help the reader follow a point
Too much of it and the reader will glaze over everything
Don’t use underline, strikethrough, or colored text. This is distracting and not intuitive for a reader
Numerals
Spell out numbers one through nine (and first through ninth)
Do this: “There are four use cases”
Not this: “There are 4 use cases”
Use numerals for 10 and above
Do this: “11 data engineers”
Not this: “Eleven data engineers”
Use numerals below 10 when writing big numbers (4 million)
For quantities less than 1, use the leading 0
Do this: 0.3%
Not this: .3%
Use a comma to separate 000s, except for years (1,234,567)
Plurals
Use s, not ‘s to pluralize abbreviations, dates, and numbers.
Do this: “The 1990s were a wild time”
Not this: “The 1990’s were a wild time”
Use fewer for countable objects/things: “Fewer than half the charts were impacted”
Use less for measurable quantities: “Less than 30% of the revenue came from new customers”
Possessives
For singular names and words that end in s, add ‘s, not just an apostrophe
Do this: “Marketing operations’s recommendations will improve efficiency”
Not this: “Marketing operations’ recommendations will improve efficiency”
For plural nouns that don’t end in s, treat with ‘s
Do this: men’s or women’s or children’s
Not this: mens or womens or childrens
Pronouns
Use “it” or “its” and not “they” or “their” when referring to organizations
Do this: “Netflix raised its revenue targets after a successful year with DataHub”
Not this: “Netflix raised their revenue targets after a successful year with DataHub”
You can use “they” or “their” when you are referring to the people in an organization
For example: “Netflix’s sales leaders increased their revenue targets”
For example: “The sales leaders met to review the pipeline. Based on the data, they decided to raise revenue targets”
Punctuation
Quotation marks
Use double quotation marks (except for a quote within a quote)
Don’t use quotation marks as “air quotes” to express irony. It makes it appear to be a quote, when it is not. That can be misleading and confusing
When the quote ends the sentence, use the ending punctuation (. or ?) inside the quotation marks
Use a comma within quotation marks when it is in the middle of a sentence.
Do this: She told me, “I don’t like broccoli,” and then she begrudgingly ate it
Not this: She told me, “I don’t like broccoli”, and then she begrudgingly ate it
Dashes
Use the ordinary hyphen for a range between numbers. For example: “10-20”
Use the em dash for breaking up a sentence, with no space on either side—like this. (on a mac the em dash is shift-option-hyphen)
Note: Use em dashes sparingly, as they have been co-opted by GenAI
Hyphens
Hyphenate words when they act as a modifier (fourth-quarter earnings, financial-services company) but not when they act as a noun (fourth quarter, financial services)
Don’t hyphenate one quarter, two thirds, third fifths, etc. unless it’s a modifier like “a two-thirds increase”
Colons
Capitalize the word after a colon if what follows is a complete sentence, but use lowercase otherwise
Do this for a complete sentence: “Remind yourself why you’re using data in the first place: You need to find answers”
Do this for a sentence fragment: “To get more insights from your data, you have to remind yourself why you’re using data in the first place: to find answers”
Semicolons
Avoid semicolons and opt for a period instead
The new sentence from a period gives the reader breathing room with a designated pause, and makes it easier to read. The new sentence makes the message more definitive and clear
In a rare instance where a semicolon must be used, don’t capitalize the word after a semicolon
Commas
Use serial commas. The Oxford “serial” comma is a comma used immediately before the coordinating conjunction ("and" or "or") in a list of three or more items
While they are not always necessary for clarity, sometimes they are. For consistency’s sake and to avoid confusion / questions, always use the serial comma. Here is a scenario in which it is necessary:
Do this: “We went walking with the dogs, grandma, and grandpa”
Not this: “We went walking with the dogs, grandma and grandpa.” (see how this example sounds like the dogs are named grandma and grandpa)
Use two commas, or none at all, when inserting a clause into a sentence.
Equally acceptable: Use two commas or none at all when inserting a clause into a sentence
Ellipsis
 A punctuation mark consisting of three dots used to indicate the omission of one or more words, a pause, or a trailing off in thought
No space before the ellipsis, one space after… like so
Exclamation marks 
Generally avoid – only use if in a quote. Exclamation points are distracting and make statements less professional or unsophisticated
Some exceptions can be made for social media (e.g. “Welcome to the team, [New Hire Name]!”
Parentheses 
Use regular parentheses ( ). When the sentence ends with parentheses, put the ending punctuation outside of the closing parenthesis. For example: “She told me a secret (though it wasn’t very exciting).”
When the parentheses is a standalone sentence, put the ending punctuation inside of the closing parenthesis. For example: “She told me a secret. (I did not find it exciting.)”
Sourcing
Always include a link to the source data if possible. If hyperlinks aren’t available, list the source name in parentheses (Gartner) 
Exception: For ebooks/guides we use footnotes for sources to streamline content
Make it so that readers can read more about the data if they want
Accessibility and Inclusion
It’s our responsibility to ensure our content is accessible and inclusive to everyone. While writing, ask yourself the following questions:
If someone couldn’t see the colors, emojis, images, or video in my content, is the message still clear?
Have I checked that I haven’t used insensitive or offensive language—even accidentally?
Is the format clean and well-structured so an accessibility device, such as a screen reader, could follow it?
Would the language I use make sense to someone who doesn’t work at DataHub?
Accessibility Guidelines
Employ a hierarchy and use headers and subheadings to separate different topics
Use descriptive links to provide information on where you’re directing the reader
Avoid “click here” or “learn more.” Instead, opt for something more specific, like “Book a demo”, or “Watch the full session on demand.”
Use alt text on all images, unless strictly decorative. The alt text should be succinct, but descriptive. A person who won’t see the image should get the same amount of information from the alt text as someone who will
When sharing a chart or graph, include the data in the alt text
Use captions in addition to alt text whenever possible
Include closed captions on videos whenever possible
Inclusive Language
We never use idioms relating to disability, age, gender, ethnicity, or religion.
Avoid gendered terms in favor of gender-neutral alternatives
Do this: “Seller” or “salesperson”
Not this: “Salesman”
Don’t refer to anyone’s disability, age, gender, ethnicity, or religion unless it’s relevant to what you are writing
For example, it is okay: In an article on women in engineering, you can reference the % of women who are VP Eng in an industry
