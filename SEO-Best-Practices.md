
Writing in 2026: Best Practices
This doc captures all the most current best practices (though these are evolving fast) for writing AEO/SEO-optimized content. Writers still need to use their judgment when writing and reconcile with brand/product positioning. Not every guideline will apply to every piece: When tradeoffs are required, prioritize clarity and retrieval over rigid adherence.

Key: 🔎 = SEO benefits | ✨= AEO benefits | ☺️= reader benefits. 
We recommend considering all of these

Secondary entity level optimization
We’ve already defined and anchored the primary entity (what the piece is primarily about), but there will be multiple related entities (think of an entity as any concept you reference that could have its own Wikipedia page).

Each of these also benefits from:
Being named explicitly
A brief clarifying sentence when first introduced
Avoiding pronouns (“it”, “this”, or “they”) in the first paragraph before the name is anchored
THIS: “An [entity] improves trust across teams by…”
NOT THIS: “This builds trust across teams by...”
Consider ‘negative clarification’—what it’s not, where there might be confusion

Tip: Definitions/callboxes or suggested as sidebars can give you a way to ‘cordon off’ 101 information if you’re writing for a more upmarket reader

H2 style
Phrase your H2s as questions when it feels natural to do so. LLMs love the question format—aim for 30-50% of H2s as questions (iff it feels right.)

Body under Hs
Try to keep each subsection visually balanced in terms of length
☺️
Body paragraphs
Try to keep paragraphs around 4 lines to better enable skim-and-scan readers
✨🔎☺️
Bullet point/numbered lists
Whenever there’s a list in a paragraph, try to break it up into bullet points for easier reading

If the sentence preceding includes a number (e.g., “there are three components to something:” then number rather than bullet the list

Similarly, if an H2 contains a number “Five benefits of X”, then number the subsequent H3s
🔎
“Step” headings
Do not write a series of H2s/H3s with the same word (e.g. Step 1:, Step 2:, etc. or Best Practice 1:, Best Practice 2:)

Instead, make the macro heading “X steps to achieve Y” and then number the nested H3s without the repetitive word.
✨🔎☺️
Tables
If you’re comparing two things or breaking down steps to accomplish something, consider whether a table can replace the paragraphs. Or whether a simplified table can be used to provide an at-a-glance overview that’s then elaborated beneath. LLMs love structured data so tables work especially well for AEO

✨🔎☺️
Sentence length
There is no hard rule on ideal sentence length (some say 22 words, but we don’t hold to this). Use common sense and avoid unnecessarily long sentences
✨
—
Em dashes are not inherently “bad”, but do be aware of LLMs tendency to overuse them. As a result, some clients may consider them an “AI tell”. Use intentionally; they often signal a sentence length issue.
✨🔎☺️
“Weasel words”
(E-E-A-T factor)
Instead of weasel words, try to get quantified data from an authoritative source, e.g. 
THIS: “According to the National Forestry Database, over 8000 fires occur each year, and burn an average of over 2.1 million hectares.” 
NOT THIS: “There are more forest fires every year” 
✨🔎☺️
Linking out externally
(E-E-A-T factor)
Try to include a minimum of 3 authoritative third-party sources in every article (more if it’s medical or regulatory)

Link out to them with the link anchored on the brand/publication, e.g.:
THIS: “According to 2024 data from the National Forestry Database, over 8000 fires occur each year, and burn an average of over 2.1 million hectares.” 
NOT THIS: “According to 2024 data from the National Forestry Database, over 8000 fires occur each year, and burn an average of over 2.1 million hectares.” 

Recency is also important for data citations (less so for standing definitions). Data should ideally be within 2-3 years, but 5 years may be more realistic in medical content. Where appropriate, include the date in your citation, as in the example above.
🔎
Linking internally
As mentioned earlier, link to your money page early. You can repeat this, as it makes sense. Also consider opportunities to interlink to other relevant blog posts, esp within cluster (check tracker or site architecture for relevant posts) or other money pages/resources.

Unlike external links, try to anchor from the KW of your destination page (or a variation of it). Avoid linking from generic phrases like “check out this blog post” and instead write “learn more about [topic]”, linking from [topic]
✨🔎☺️
SME Quotes
(don’t over-suggest)
(E-E-A-T factor)
If appropriate for the client, suggest some SME quotes that can be woven into the piece and add more voice and original perspective to the piece. This can especially be used to incorporate the ‘voice/product perspective’ on information. Attribute suggestions to “- SME, [Company]” and let the client decide internally who to attribute these to.

Notes:
Pull quotes are generally preferred to inline quotes for skim and scan and visual treatment. 
Highlight these green so the client can readily see in the doc.
✨🔎☺️
Product incorporation
In general, don’t just wait until the very end of the piece to throw to product. Be proud of your product!!

Weave it in clearly throughout the piece but be mindful of where in the funnel the piece ‘sits’ and avoid being salesy about it. E.g., this can be done with fact boxes, “How [Brand] Helps” in sections that describe problems. 
✨🔎
FAQs
FAQs are ideal for LLMs and while not every client wants them, they’re a great way to ‘declutter’ the main body. FAQ suggestions can be derived from Surfer, AI checklists from our team and asking LLMs for suggestions that specifically pertain to the product.

Generally cap it at 8-10 FAQs and balance more SEO minded informational questions (that might drive AIO but not clicks), and more BOFU product specific FAQs (how does [brand] do this)

Formatting:
Section H2: FAQs
H3s: The questions
Body: The answers
✨🔎
FAQ Answers
Lead with a 1–2 sentence direct answer, then elaborate
40–70 words for informational FAQs
60–120 words for product or implementation FAQs
✨🔎
Overall length
There is no ideal target length that applies to all content. Take the time to answer the topic at hand and do so without unnecessary fluff. Use FAQs to tuck away information without adding to page length.

Content is trending longer: 2500 words is now ‘normal’. 3500-4000 words is no longer unusual.

The most important consideration:
Longer content performs better when it introduces genuinely distinct retrieval chunks
Long content that repeats itself is actively worse for AEO

In general, content should be optimized every 12 months (realistically every 18 months). Top converting pages can be revisited much more frequently.

Technical Docs: Specific Guidance
Many of the SEO/AEO best practices documented above carry over to technical documentation. However, for docs, the emphasis and specific tactics differ slightly because the audience, purpose, and discovery mechanisms for docs are not the same as for blog content. 

Public-facing technical docs can benefit from SEO/AEO optimization, but the core writing quality, accuracy, and usability take priority.
Key Differences in Focus Between Blog Content + Docs
Focus
Blog Content
Technical Docs
Purpose
attract, inform, convert
instruct, empower, reference
Tone
engaging, conversational
clear, authoritative, precise
Structure
narrative + entity clusters
task flows + examples
Discovery
organic search + AI snippets
search + deep linking + user intent in queries


Technical docs mindset:
Task over “click”: Your blog best practices emphasize why someone clicked. In docs, the user already clicked for purpose: To solve a task, integrate, fix something.
Search behavior is fundamentally different: Doc searches are often extremely specific (error messages, API method names, exact config keys). Optimize for those long-tail queries, not broad keywords.

The AEO angle for docs
Developers increasingly ask LLMs "how do I configure X in [Platform]" rather than reading docs. The same entity-optimization principles apply—maybe more so—because LLMs are synthesizing across your docs. 
Clean definitions, unambiguous terminology, and explicit naming of concepts all help your docs become the source LLMs cite.

Different doc types
Procedural docs (how to do something): Procedural docs need the prerequisites/steps/outcomes structure described below
Conceptual docs (what something is): Closer to blog content in structure
Reference docs (API specs, parameter tables): Are almost entirely tables and code (not all the following applies)
Note re auto-generated API documentation: If you use OpenAPI/Swagger, the docs are auto-generated and writers aren't touching them. The optimization happens when developers write the OpenAPI spec. That's a developer education issue, not a content writing issue.

Prerequisites sections
Every task-based doc should open with what the user needs before starting (permissions, installed dependencies, prior configuration).
​​THIS: Prerequisites:
Admin permissions in your [Platform] instance
Python 3.8+ installed
API key generated (see Authentication)
NOT THIS: "Before you begin, make sure you have the right permissions and have set everything up correctly."
✨☺️
Entity optimization throughout (no weasel words)
Specificity is actually even more important in docs. Every concept, feature, API endpoint, and configuration option should be named explicitly and defined on first use. Docs are reference material; ambiguity is fatal.
✨🔎☺️
H2s, H3s, H4s
Good structure is key for all content, use H-styles to organize content logically to flow. Use task-based headings in Docs.

Think of the content under each H as somewhat self-contained for both LLMs and scan and skim readers. Frame sections around common user queries (“How do I use the API to fetch X?”) to align with search intent.

Structuring headings around user tasks or questions (e.g., “How to authenticate”) boosts findability.

Follow client’s style guide in terms of casing for H-styles. Use https://capitalizemytitle.com/# if title case
☺️✨🔎
Body paragraphs
More aggressively short here: Try to keep paragraphs around 2 lines to better enable skim-and-scan readers who are looking for quick answers.
✨🔎☺️
Procedural formatting / Bullet point/numbered lists
Steps should be numbered, one action per step, with expected outcomes. 
THIS:
Navigate to Settings > Integrations.
Click Add Source. The source configuration modal appears.
Select Snowflake from the dropdown.
NOT THIS: "Go to Settings, then Integrations, and click Add Source. You'll see a modal pop up where you can select Snowflake from the dropdown menu to proceed."


✨🔎☺️
Writing style
Write in active voice
Use precise technical terminology
Zero fluff or editorializing
✨🔎☺️
Tables
Essential for parameter references, comparison matrices, configuration options. 

LLMs love structured data so tables work especially well for AEO

[Note to client: Do not make tables into images as this strips them of their benefits]
🔎☺️
Graphic opportunities 
Marketing imagery is not important in docs, but clearly legible UI screenshots or gifs can help user comprehension of steps.

Ensure alt text accurately describes what is contained in the image and images are compressed for speed
✨🔎☺️
Code-centred content
Code-centered content should be treated as structured data: Technical docs often contain code examples, parameters, and specs. 

These behave differently than prose for search and AI:
Meaningful names inside code examples help discovery (search engines index commented code blocks).
Keep syntax highlighting, consistent formatting, and plain text code blocks (Markdown).
✨🔎
Schema markup & metadata
Even for docs, title tags, meta descriptions, and FAQ/HowTo schema improve discoverability.
JSON-LD for API definitions and HowTo steps can boost AI/voice retrieval.
✨☺️
Recency signals
Version numbers, "last updated" dates, and compatibility notes matter enormously. 

Version control ensures instructions match the current release. Some docs platforms support URL versioning (/v1.0/..., /v2.1/...), preventing confusion.

This is a technical documentation best practice rather than an SEO requirement, but it directly affects user trust and search accuracy.
✨🔎☺️
Consider error states and troubleshooting
What happens when it doesn't work? This is where most docs fail. Each procedural doc should anticipate 2-3 common failure modes.
THIS: Troubleshooting Connection refused on port 8080: Verify the DataHub server is running (docker ps) and the port isn't blocked by your firewall.
NOT THIS: "If something goes wrong, check your configuration and try again.
✨🔎☺️
Information architecture within posts
Blog posts are standalone; docs are part of a system. Navigation, cross-linking, and "next steps" matter as much as any individual page's optimization.

Link to prerequisite docs at the top, related tasks inline, and next logical steps at the bottom. Anchor from the action the user will take, not generic phrases.
THIS: "Before configuring ingestion, set up authentication."
NOT THIS: "Before configuring ingestion, click here for setup instructions."


Other docs writing/optimization steps

✨🔎
Overarching documentation architecture
Docs often have huge content sets (e.g., sidebar with APIs, SDKs, concepts, etc.). Good navigation is core UX: Top-level categorization (e.g., “Getting Started”, “Concepts”, “APIs”, “Guides”) reduces friction.
☺️
Feedback & analytics loops
Technical docs benefit greatly from real-user feedback: “Was this page helpful?” widgets help you spot gaps and identify opportunities for additional resources
